Lab Introduction

This lab introduces you to sedimentary rocks, the record of Earth's surface environments. By understanding how these rocks form, you can read them like pages in a book, extracting information about ancient climates, landscapes, and oceans.

Sedimentary rocks and sediments cover ~70% of Earth's surface, yet they represent less than 1% of Earth's total volume, a thin but information-rich "surface veneer."

All clastic sedimentary rocks begin with the breakdown of pre-existing rock through weathering. Understanding how rocks weather helps explain the textures and mineralogy of the sedimentary rocks they become.

The Clastic Sedimentary Path

Clastic (detrital) sedimentary rocks form through a chain of surface processes:

Parent Rock
→
Weathering
→
Erosion / Transport
→
Deposition
→
Lithification

Transport agents include water, wind, ice (glaciers), and gravity. Each agent leaves distinctive textural signatures in the resulting rock.

Physical (Mechanical) Weathering

Physical weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces without changing its chemistry. Key processes include:

Pressure Unloading (Exfoliation) +

When overlying rock is removed by erosion, rocks that formed under great pressure can expand and fracture parallel to the surface. This produces exfoliation domes (e.g., Half Dome in Yosemite) and joint-bounded blocks.

The removal of overburden allows the rock to "rebound", sheets peel off like layers of an onion.

Frost Wedging +

Water seeps into cracks, freezes, and expands by ~9% in volume. This wedges the crack wider. Repeated freeze-thaw cycles progressively break the rock apart, familiar to anyone who has seen potholes in Pennsylvania roads or frost-shattered rocks in the mountains.

Most effective in climates with frequent freeze-thaw cycles; produces angular fragments (no transport = no rounding).

Biologic Action +

Living organisms can physically break down rock: tree roots growing into cracks, burrowing animals, even herds of grazing animals compacting and disturbing soil. These are physical processes, no chemical change required.

Key insight: Physical weathering increases surface area without changing composition, which then accelerates chemical weathering!

Chemical Weathering

Chemical weathering changes the mineral composition of rock through reactions with water, oxygen, and acids:

ProcessWhat HappensExample
DissolutionMineral breaks into ions in waterLimestone dissolving in rainwater
OxidationReaction with free oxygen alters the mineralIron → Hematite (rust-red color)
HydrolysisH⁺ or OH⁻ replaces an ion in the mineralFeldspar → Clay minerals
LeachingIons are removed by moving waterNa⁺, K⁺ carried away from granite
DehydrationRemoval of H⁺ or OH⁻ ion from mineralGoethite → Hematite

Mineral Stability During Weathering

Minerals crystallize at different temperatures in Bowen's Reaction Series. Minerals that form at the highest temperatures (olivine, pyroxene) are least stable at the Earth's surface and weather fastest. Minerals that form last (quartz) are the most stable.

OlivineVery Low — weathers fastestClay minerals + Mg²⁺ ions
Lab implication: If a sandstone contains fresh feldspar grains, those grains must not have traveled far from their source (otherwise they would have weathered away). This tells you about transport distance!

Identifying a sedimentary rock follows a systematic process. The first step is always texture - what does the rock look like at the grain scale? This determines which ID table you use.

Types of Sedimentary Rocks

Clastic / Detrital
Made of physical sediment fragments (rock/mineral grains) deposited by erosion and transport. Classified by grain size and shape.
Chemical
Precipitated directly from solution (ions in water). Classified primarily by composition.
Biochemical
Chemical sediments formed or concentrated by organisms (shells, skeletons, plant material). Classified by composition and texture.

Texture-Based ID Decision Tree

Always start here - this tells you which classification table to use.

Step 1: Are grains visible? +

YES → The grains are distinct. Proceed to Step 2.

NO → The rock has a fine-grained clastic or microcrystalline texture. Proceed to the fine-grained decision tree (Step 4).

Step 2: Are grains intergrown or distinct? +

Intergrown (locked together, no open spaces) → Crystalline texture. Use the crystalline ID path (Step 3a).

Distinct from each other → Clastic texture. Classify by grain size, shape, composition using Table 4.4 (Clastic Rocks tab).

Fossils or fossil fragments visible → Rock is fossiliferous. Likely limestone, dolostone, or coal. Use Table 4.5 (Chemical Rocks tab).

Step 3a: Crystalline texture - hardness/acid tests +

Easily scratched by fingernail → Rock Gypsum (hardness ~2)

Harder than fingernail, softer than penny, tastes salty → Rock Salt (Halite)

~Hardness of a penny + vigorous fizz in dilute HCl → Limestone

~Hardness of a penny + slow/weak fizz in dilute HCl → Dolostone

Step 4: Fine-grained / microcrystalline texture +

Scratches glass + conchoidal fracture + doesn't crumble → Chert, Flint, or Jasper

Black, low density, combustible → Coal

~Hardness of penny + vigorous fizz in HCl → Limestone (micritic)

~Hardness of penny + slow fizz in HCl → Dolostone

Feels gritty on teeth + scratches glass → Siltstone

Feels smooth on teeth → Claystone; breaks with conchoidal fracture → Mudstone; fissile (splits in layers) → Shale

Easily scratched by fingernail → Rock Gypsum

Softer than penny + salty taste → Rock Salt

Clastic rocks are defined by the physical properties of their grains. Learning to assess grain size, roundness, and sorting is the core skill for identifying and interpreting these rocks.

Practical test: Rub the rock gently on your teeth. Gritty = silt-sized grains (siltstone). Smooth = clay-sized particles (claystone, shale). Coarser grains (sand and above) are visible to the eye or under a hand lens.

Grain Roundness

Roundness reflects how far a grain has traveled. Angular grains have sharp edges and come from near-source or glacial environments. Well-rounded grains have traveled long distances and experienced extensive abrasion.

TermDescriptionInterpretation
Very AngularSharp, jagged edges; no rounding visibleLittle to no transport (rockfall, glacial)
AngularSharp edges with slight wearShort transport distance
SubangularSome rounding, but corners still sharpModerate transport
SubroundedCorners rounded, but faces still flatSignificant transport
RoundedSmooth curves; no sharp cornersLong transport distance
Well RoundedNearly spherical; completely smoothVery long transport (aeolian/fluvial)

Grain Sorting

Sorting describes how uniform the grain sizes are. Well-sorted sediment (all grains the same size) indicates prolonged or selective transport. Poorly sorted sediment indicates rapid deposition with little selection.

SortingDescriptionTypical Environment
Very Well SortedNearly identical grain sizesBeach, aeolian (dune)
Well SortedGrains mostly one sizeBeach, river channel (reworked)
Moderately SortedSome grain size variationMost river environments
Poorly SortedWide range of grain sizes mixedAlluvial fan, glacial outwash
Very Poorly SortedExtreme grain size range; boulders to clayGlacial till, debris flow

Chemical and biochemical sedimentary rocks form from precipitation out of solution or from the accumulation of organic material. Composition - not grain size - drives their classification.

Limestone: CaCO₃

Limestone is the most common chemical/biochemical rock. It is composed of calcite (CaCO₃) or aragonite, and fizzes vigorously when dilute HCl is applied. It comes in many textures:

TypeAppearanceHow It Formed
Crystalline LimestoneInterlocking calcite crystals; sugary textureRecrystallization of earlier carbonate material
Micritic LimestoneDense, microcrystalline; gray to dark; conchoidal fractureAccumulation of fine carbonate mud
Oolitic LimestoneSand-like spheres (ooids) visible; grainy texturePrecipitation around grains in warm, agitated water
Fossiliferous LimestoneAbundant fossils or fossil fragments in crystalline/micritic matrixAccumulation of shell material in carbonate seas
CoquinaLoosely cemented shell fragments; coarse textureAccumulated shell material with little fine matrix
ChalkWhite, soft, powdery; earthyAccumulation of microscopic carbonate organisms (coccoliths, foraminifera) on deep sea floor
Remember: All of these are limestone - CaCO₃. The subtype names describe the texture and origin, but the acid test works on all of them.

Dolostone (Dolomite Rock)

Dolostone is composed of the mineral dolomite - CaMg(CO₃)₂. It is similar to limestone but:

  • Fizzes weakly with dilute HCl (powder fizzes more strongly)
  • Often slightly harder and denser than limestone
  • May contain small crystal-lined cavities
  • Typically forms through chemical alteration of limestone after deposition

Siliceous Rocks: SiO₂

Chert, Flint, and Jasper are all forms of microcrystalline quartz (SiO₂). They have:

  • Hardness = 7 (will scratch glass easily)
  • Conchoidal (shell-like) fracture
  • Very dense; does not crumble
  • Does not fizz with HCl

Chert = gray to white; Jasper = red/brown/yellow (iron staining); Flint = dark gray to black (often found in chalk deposits)

Watch out: Chert nodules embedded within limestone will scratch glass even though the surrounding limestone will not. If part of your sample scratches glass but the rest doesn't, test both parts with acid to distinguish them.

Evaporites

Evaporites form when water evaporates from a restricted basin, concentrating dissolved ions until minerals precipitate. Common in arid environments, playa lakes, and restricted marine settings.

Sedimentary structures are physical features preserved in the rock that record conditions at the time of deposition. They are our primary tool for interpreting ancient environments.

Primary Sedimentary Structures

These features form during or immediately after deposition. They survive lithification and can be observed in outcrops and hand specimens.

Bedding / Stratification +

The most fundamental sedimentary structure. Beds (strata) represent individual depositional events. Governed by Steno's Laws:

  • Superposition: In an undisturbed sequence, younger beds lie above older beds
  • Original Horizontality: Beds are deposited horizontally; tilted beds have been deformed
  • Lateral Continuity: Beds extend laterally until they thin out or reach the edge of the basin
Cross-Bedding +

Inclined layers within a larger bed, formed by migrating bedforms (dunes, ripples). The inclined layers dip in the direction of current flow, this is a powerful paleocurrent indicator.

Large-scale cross-beds (meters thick) → aeolian (wind) dunes. Small-scale cross-beds → subaqueous ripples or small dunes in rivers or shallow marine settings.

Graded Bedding +

A single bed that grades from coarser grains at the base to finer grains at the top. Forms when a turbulent current slows down, larger grains settle first, smaller grains later.

Classic indicator of turbidite deposits (turbidity currents on continental slopes), though also found in other high-energy, waning flow environments.

Ripple Marks +

Current ripples are asymmetric, gentle upstream side, steep downstream side. Direction of steeper side = current direction.

Wave ripples are symmetric, equal slopes on both sides. Indicate oscillating wave energy, typically shallow marine or lacustrine.

Mudcracks (Desiccation Cracks) +

Polygonal cracks formed when wet mud dries out and shrinks. A reliable indicator of subaerial exposure, the sediment was above water long enough to dry out.

Common in floodplains, tidal flats, and playa lakes. When lithified, the cracks may be filled with a different material, preserved as a pattern in the rock surface.

Fossils +

Preserved remains or traces of organisms. Fossils constrain depositional environment (marine vs. freshwater vs. terrestrial), water depth, climate, and age.

Marine fossils (corals, brachiopods, crinoids) indicate open marine conditions. Plant fossils suggest terrestrial or swampy environments. Freshwater bivalves and fish indicate lacustrine settings.

Key point: Sedimentary structures are often the only evidence we have for interpreting the depositional environment of ancient rocks. Always note their presence when examining a rock outcrop.

The ultimate goal of sedimentary rock analysis is interpretation: what does this rock tell us about where and how it formed? Different depositional environments leave distinctive rock assemblages.

Fluvial (River) System in Detail

Rivers deposit different sediments in different parts of their system. This is a key concept for interpreting fluvial rock sequences:

  • Within the channel: Larger grains (sand, gravel) settle out of the fast-moving water. Produces cross-bedded sandstone and conglomerate.
  • On the floodplain (during floods): Fine muds and clays are carried by the overflowing water and settle slowly. Produces mudstone and shale with possible root traces and mudcracks (when dry season follows).
  • An alternating sequence of coarser channel deposits and finer floodplain deposits is a characteristic signature of ancient river systems.
